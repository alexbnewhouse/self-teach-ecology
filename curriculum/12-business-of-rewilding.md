# Module 12: The Business Side of Restoration and Rewilding

**Type**: Tier 4 applied module  
**Estimated time**: 40–60 hours  
**Prerequisites**: Modules 1–8, at least one Tier 2 track complete, capstone in progress or complete  
**Assessment**: Market landscape analysis + revenue model memo + case study portfolio  
**Benchmark mapping**: Extends beyond standard ecology master's; bridging to entrepreneurship and conservation economics

---

## Purpose

Most ecology programs stop at research and policy. This module fills the gap between ecological expertise and the practical economics of turning that knowledge into products, services, and sustainable businesses. You will study how restoration and rewilding ventures actually make money, where the market gaps are, and how ecological rigor intersects with consumer-facing products.

This is **preparation** for Module 13 (product development). Do not skip it—your product ideas will be dramatically better if you understand the landscape first.

---

## Learning Objectives

1. Map the current commercial landscape of restoration, native plant, and ecological gardening businesses
2. Understand revenue models used by conservation-tech startups, native plant nurseries, and ecological service providers
3. Evaluate where market gaps exist at the intersection of ecology, technology, and consumer behavior
4. Analyze the ecosystem services valuation framework and how it connects to product value propositions
5. Distinguish between mission-driven and profit-driven approaches to ecological products and identify your own positioning
6. Develop basic fluency in product-market fit, lean startup methodology, and sustainable business models

## Unit 12.1: Conservation Economics and Ecosystem Services

### Readings

**Foundational**:
> Daily, G.C. (Ed.) (1997). *Nature's Services: Societal Dependence on Natural Ecosystems*. Island Press.
> - Chapters 1–3 and 10–12: The intellectual foundation for why ecological work has economic value

> TEEB (2010). *The Economics of Ecosystems and Biodiversity: Mainstreaming the Economics of Nature*. UNEP.
> - Free PDF. Focus on the synthesis chapter and case studies
> - http://teebweb.org/publications/

> Costanza, R., et al. (2014). Changes in the global value of ecosystem services. *Global Environmental Change*, 26, 152–158.
> - Updated valuation that puts hard numbers on ecosystem service categories

**Applied context**:
> BenDor, T., Lester, T.W., Livengood, A., Davis, A., & Yonavjak, L. (2015). Estimating the size and impact of the ecological restoration economy. *PLoS ONE*, 10(6), e0128339.
> - Landmark paper: The US ecological restoration economy supports ~126,000 jobs and $9.5 billion in output
> - Key insight: This is already a real industry, not a niche

> Blignaut, J., Aronson, J., & de Groot, R. (2014). Restoration of natural capital: A key strategy on the path to sustainability. *Ecological Engineering*, 65, 54–61.

### Key Concepts
- Ecosystem services categories: provisioning, regulating, cultural, supporting
- Natural capital accounting
- Payment for ecosystem services (PES) schemes
- The restoration economy as a jobs driver
- Willingness to pay for ecological outcomes vs. ecological products

### Exercise 12.1: Ecosystem Services Inventory
Pick a specific habitat type in your region (e.g., tallgrass prairie, riparian buffer, coastal wetland). Catalog the ecosystem services it provides using the TEEB categories. Estimate rough economic value ranges using published literature. Write a 1-page value proposition: "If someone restored 1 acre of [habitat], here's the value created."

## Unit 12.2: Market Landscape — Who's Already Doing This?

### Research Tasks (Self-Directed)

Spend 8–12 hours surveying the current market. Document your findings in a structured landscape analysis.

**Categories to investigate**:

1. **Native plant nurseries and seed companies**
   - Examples: Prairie Moon Nursery, Ernst Conservation Seeds, Roundstone Native Seed, local equivalents
   - Questions: How do they price? What's their value proposition? Do they offer guidance, or just sell plants?

2. **Ecological garden design and consulting**
   - Examples: Larry Weaner Landscape Associates, local native plant landscape designers
   - Questions: Service model vs. product model? Scalability?

3. **Conservation tech and data platforms**
   - Examples: iNaturalist, Seek (by iNaturalist), PictureThis, PlantNet, Budburst
   - Questions: What data do they collect? How do they engage users? Revenue model?

4. **Smart garden and IoT products**
   - Examples: Gardyn, AeroGarden, various smart irrigation systems, soil sensors
   - Questions: What ecological intelligence do these lack? Who are they designed for?

5. **Rewilding-adjacent consumer products**
   - Examples: Yardmap (Cornell Lab), Homegrown National Park (Doug Tallamy initiative), Wild Ones chapters
   - Questions: What's missing from these? Where do they stop short?

6. **Restoration service businesses**
   - Examples: Applied Ecological Services, Biohabitats, Cardno (now Stantec)
   - Questions: What's their business model? B2B vs. B2C? Can elements be productized?

7. **Emerging and niche players**
   - Look for: backyard biodiversity certification programs, ecological data tracking apps, native plant subscription boxes, restoration-as-a-service startups

### Deliverable: Market Landscape Document
A 3,000–5,000 word document with:
- Category-by-category findings
- At least 20 companies/products analyzed
- Identified gaps (what no one is doing well)
- Consumer pain points you've observed
- A preliminary "opportunity map" showing underserved intersections

## Unit 12.3: Revenue Models for Ecological Products

### Readings

> Osterwalder, A. & Pigneur, Y. (2010). *Business Model Generation*. Wiley.
> - Read the Canvas section (pp. 14–51) and pattern section (pp. 52–119)
> - You do not need the whole book—focus on the visual framework

> Ries, E. (2011). *The Lean Startup*. Crown Business.
> - Chapters 1–8: Core loop of Build → Measure → Learn
> - Critical for ecological products where you don't yet know what the user wants

**Conservation entrepreneurship**:
> Kareiva, P. & Marvier, M. (2012). What is conservation science? *BioScience*, 62(11), 962–969.
> - The "new conservation" argument for market-engaged approaches

> Soulé, M. (2013). The "new conservation." *Conservation Biology*, 27(5), 895–897.
> - The counter-argument: Be cautious about market logic in conservation

### Revenue Model Taxonomy for Ecological Products

Study and categorize:

| Model | Examples | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Freemium app** | iNaturalist, Seek | Massive reach, data network effects | Hard to monetize, needs scale |
| **Subscription** | Plant care apps, seed-of-the-month | Recurring revenue, community | Churn risk, content treadmill |
| **Marketplace/e-commerce** | Native plant sales, kit bundles | Clear value exchange | Inventory/logistics complexity |
| **SaaS / data platform** | Farm management software, ecological consultants | High margins at scale | Long sales cycles, B2B complexity |
| **Content / education** | Online courses, guidebooks | Low marginal cost | Crowded space, hard to differentiate |
| **Hardware + software** | Smart sensors, monitoring kits | High lock-in, bundled value | Manufacturing complexity, capital needs |
| **Consulting + productized service** | Site assessments, planting plans | Premium pricing | Time-for-money trap, hard to scale |
| **Community + certification** | Wildlife habitat certifications, badges | Mission alignment, viral | Low direct revenue per user |

### Exercise 12.3: Revenue Model Canvas
Pick three potential product directions (don't commit—just explore). For each, fill out a simplified Business Model Canvas:
- Value proposition
- Customer segments
- Channels
- Revenue streams
- Key resources
- Cost structure

Write a 1-page memo comparing the three. Which has the best combination of mission impact and revenue sustainability?

## Unit 12.4: The Native Plant Gardening Opportunity

### Why This Matters

Doug Tallamy's work (*Nature's Best Hope*, *Bringing Nature Home*) has created massive consumer awareness of native plant gardening. But infrastructure hasn't caught up:

- Most people don't know **which** native plants to choose for their specific conditions
- There's almost no **data tracking** for backyard ecological outcomes
- Smart garden tech ignores ecological complexity entirely
- Guidance doesn't scale with land size (a balcony ≠ a 5-acre plot)

### Readings

> Tallamy, D.W. (2007). *Bringing Nature Home*. Timber Press.
> - The book that launched the native plant gardening movement. Read at least chapters 1–5.

> Tallamy, D.W. (2019). *Nature's Best Hope*. Timber Press.
> - The vision for "Homegrown National Park." Understand the movement you'd be building within.

> Narango, D.L., Tallamy, D.W., & Marra, P.P. (2018). Nonnative plants reduce population growth of an insectivorous bird. *PNAS*, 115(45), 11549–11554.
> - The empirical backbone: Native plants quantifiably support more bird life

> Burghardt, K.T., Tallamy, D.W., & Shriver, W.G. (2009). Impact of native plants on bird and butterfly biodiversity in suburban landscapes. *Conservation Biology*, 23(1), 219–224.

> National Wildlife Federation. Certified Wildlife Habitat program.
> - https://www.nwf.org/Garden-for-Wildlife — Study how they've productized certification

### Key Market Gaps to Interrogate

As you go through the curriculum, keep a running log of these questions:

1. **Personalized plant selection**: Can ecological niche modeling (Module 4) be applied to backyard-scale recommendations?
2. **Outcome tracking**: What would a "biodiversity dashboard" for a home garden look like? What metrics matter?
3. **Scale adaptation**: How does guidance change from a container garden to a half-acre yard to a 20-acre property?
4. **Seasonal management**: What does an ecologically-informed maintenance calendar look like vs. traditional gardening advice?
5. **Community data**: Could aggregated user data create citizen-science value? (See eBird model)
6. **Integration with smart home**: Soil moisture sensors + species phenology data + weather APIs = ?

### Exercise 12.4: Gap Analysis
Write a 2-page analysis picking ONE of the gaps above. Use knowledge from your completed modules to argue:
- Why existing solutions are insufficient (cite ecological principles)
- What a better solution would need to include
- What data sources from [datasets-and-tools.md](../resources/datasets-and-tools.md) could power it

## Unit 12.5: Case Studies in Conservation Entrepreneurship

### Case Study Portfolio

Research and write brief (500–800 word) case studies on at least **four** of the following:

1. **iNaturalist** — How a free platform became the world's largest biodiversity data source. Revenue: grants + institutional partnerships, not users.
2. **Ecosia** — Search engine funding reforestation. Model: advertising revenue → tree planting.
3. **Restor (ETH Zurich spin-off)** — Connecting restoration science to ground-level action. How they bridge data and implementation.
4. **Prairie Moon Nursery** — From small farm to leading native plant supplier. Scaling a niche ecological product.
5. **BeeHome / Polly by Beewise** — Smart beehive technology. Hardware + ecological intelligence.
6. **Land Life Company** — Technology-enabled reforestation at scale. Venture-backed restoration.
7. **Biohabitats** — Ecological consulting firm. How expertise becomes a service business.
8. **Homegrown National Park** — Movement-building as a platform. Tallamy's non-product product.
9. **Wildflower Turf Ltd (UK)** — Pre-grown wildflower meadow sod. Physical product solving an ecological problem.
10. **Terso / Seedstars** — Conservation-focused accelerator programs. What they fund and why.

For each case study, analyze:
- What problem they solve
- Revenue model
- Ecological credibility (or lack thereof)
- What they do well and what's missing
- Lessons for your own product thinking
