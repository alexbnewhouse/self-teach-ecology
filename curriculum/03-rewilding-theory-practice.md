# Module 3: Rewilding — Theory & Practice

**Estimated time**: 60–80 hours  
**Prerequisites**: Module 2  
**Parallelizable with**: Modules 4–5 (after completing Module 2)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

1. Trace the intellectual history of rewilding from wilderness preservation to contemporary practice
2. Distinguish among different rewilding frameworks (Pleistocene rewilding, trophic rewilding, passive rewilding, translocation rewilding)
3. Evaluate the ecological evidence for rewilding, including trophic cascades and ecosystem recovery
4. Critically assess the social, political, and ethical dimensions of rewilding projects
5. Analyze case studies of rewilding initiatives worldwide
6. Connect rewilding theory to your understanding of conservation biology, governance, and data science

## Unit 3.1: Intellectual Foundations of Rewilding

### Readings

**Essential starting point**:
> Soulé, M., & Noss, R. (1998). Rewilding and biodiversity: Complementary goals for continental conservation. *Wild Earth*, 8(3), 18–28.
> - **The** paper that coined the term "rewilding" in its modern ecological sense
> - Introduced the "3 Cs": Cores, Corridors, and Carnivores

**Historical context**:
> Foreman, D. (2004). *Rewilding North America: A Vision for Conservation in the 21st Century*. Island Press.
> - Chapters 1–5, 10–13
> - The activist-conservationist perspective on rewilding

> Cronon, W. (1996). The trouble with wilderness: Or, getting back to the wrong nature. *Environmental History*, 1(1), 7–28.
> - Essential critique of the "wilderness" concept that underlies some rewilding thinking

> Jørgensen, D. (2015). Rethinking rewilding. *Geoforum*, 65, 482–488. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.geoforum.2014.11.016
> - Thoughtful analysis of the conceptual ambiguity in "rewilding"

**Evolution of the concept**:
> Lorimer, J., Sandom, C., Jepson, P., Doughty, C., Barua, M., & Kirby, K.J. (2015). Rewilding: Science, practice, and politics. *Annual Review of Environment and Resources*, 40, 39–62. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-environ-102014-021406
> - **Highly recommended**: The most comprehensive review of rewilding as a field

> Prior, J., & Ward, K.J. (2016). Rethinking rewilding: A response to Jørgensen. *Geoforum*, 69, 132–135.

### Key Concepts
- The "3 Cs" framework (Cores, Corridors, Carnivores)
- Rewilding vs. restoration: conceptual distinctions
- Wildness as a continuum, not a binary
- Baseline debates: What are we "re-wilding" toward?
- The Anthropocene context and "novel ecosystems"

### Exercise 3.1: Conceptual Mapping
Create a concept map or timeline showing how rewilding has evolved as an idea:
1. Start with the American wilderness movement (Muir, Leopold, the Wilderness Act of 1964)
2. Map through Soulé & Noss (1998) and the Wildlands Project
3. Include the Pleistocene rewilding debate (Donlan et al., 2005/2006)
4. Show the European rewilding turn (Rewilding Europe, 2011+)

**Deliverable**: A visual concept map (digital or hand-drawn) with brief annotations.

## Unit 3.2: Trophic Rewilding

### Readings

**Core framework paper**:
> Svenning, J.-C., Pedersen, P.B.M., Donlan, C.J., Ejrnæs, R., Faurby, S., Galetti, M., Hansen, D.M., Sandel, B., Sandom, C.J., Terborgh, J.W., & Vera, F.W.M. (2016). Science for a wilder Anthropocene: Synthesis and future directions for trophic rewilding research. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 113(4), 898–906. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1502556112
> - **Essential**: Defines trophic rewilding as a distinct paradigm

**Trophic cascades evidence**:
> Estes, J.A., Terborgh, J., Brashares, J.S., Power, M.E., Berger, J., Bond, W.J., ... & Wardle, D.A. (2011). Trophic downgrading of planet Earth. *Science*, 333(6040), 301–306. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1205106
> - Landmark synthesis on the global consequences of losing large predators

> Ripple, W.J., & Beschta, R.L. (2012). Trophic cascades in Yellowstone: The first 15 years after wolf reintroduction. *Biological Conservation*, 145(1), 205–213. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biocon.2011.11.005

> Ripple, W.J., Estes, J.A., Beschta, R.L., Wilmers, C.C., Ritchie, E.G., Hebblewhite, M., ... & Wirsing, A.J. (2014). Status and ecological effects of the world's largest carnivores. *Science*, 343(6167), 1241484.

**Herbivore rewilding**:
> Bakker, E.S., Gill, J.L., Johnson, C.N., Vera, F.W.M., Sandom, C.J., Asner, G.P., & Svenning, J.-C. (2016). Combining paleo-data and modern exclosure experiments to assess the impact of megafauna extinctions on woody vegetation. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 113(4), 847–855.

> Vera, F.W.M. (2000). *Grazing Ecology and Forest History*. CABI Publishing.
> - Chapters 1–4; the controversial but influential "wood-pasture hypothesis"

### Key Concepts
- Trophic cascades: top-down regulation of ecosystems
- Mesopredator release
- Herbivore-mediated landscape heterogeneity
- Megafauna defaunation and its ecosystem consequences
- The wood-pasture hypothesis (Vera)
- Functional extinction vs. numerical extinction
- Trophic complexity and ecosystem resilience

### Exercise 3.2: Yellowstone Trophic Cascade Analysis
The wolf reintroduction in Yellowstone (1995) is the most studied rewilding trophic cascade. Critically evaluate the evidence:
1. Read Ripple & Beschta (2012) and at least two critiques or alternative analyses:
   - Kauffman, M.J., Brodie, J.F., & Jules, E.S. (2010). Are wolves saving Yellowstone's aspen? *Ecology*, 91(9), 2742–2755.
   - Painter, L.E., Beschta, R.L., Larsen, E.J., & Ripple, W.J. (2015). Recovering aspen follow changing elk dynamics in Yellowstone. *Forest Ecology and Management*, 338, 142–152.
2. Write a 1,500-word critical review: How strong is the evidence for a wolf-driven trophic cascade in Yellowstone? What alternative explanations exist?

**Deliverable**: Critical review essay with citations.

## Unit 3.3: Pleistocene Rewilding and De-Extinction

### Readings

**The provocative proposal**:
> Donlan, C.J., Berger, J., Bock, C.E., Bock, J.H., Burney, D.A., Estes, J.A., ... & Greene, H.W. (2006). Pleistocene rewilding: An optimistic agenda for twenty-first century conservation. *The American Naturalist*, 168(5), 660–681. https://doi.org/10.1086/508027

> Donlan, C.J., Greene, H.W., Berger, J., Bock, C.E., Bock, J.H., Burney, D.A., ... & Estes, J.A. (2005). Re-wilding North America. *Nature*, 436(7053), 913–914.

**Responses and critiques**:
> Rubenstein, D.R., Rubenstein, D.I., Sherman, P.W., & Gavin, T.A. (2006). Pleistocene Park: Does re-wilding North America represent sound conservation for the 21st century? *Biological Conservation*, 132(2), 232–238.

> Oliveira-Santos, L.G.R., & Fernandez, F.A.S. (2010). Pleistocene rewilding, Frankenstein ecosystems, and an alternative conservation agenda. *Conservation Biology*, 24(1), 4–5.

**De-extinction**:
> Seddon, P.J., Moehrenschlager, A., & Ewen, J. (2014). Reintroducing resurrected species: Selecting DeExtinction candidates. *Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 29(3), 140–147.

> Shapiro, B. (2015). *How to Clone a Mammoth: The Science of De-Extinction*. Princeton University Press.
> - Accessible book-length treatment

> Jones, K.E. (2014). From dinosaurs to dodos: Who could and should we de-extinct? *Frontiers of Biogeography*, 6(1).

### Key Concepts
- Late Quaternary megafauna extinctions
- Ecological proxies and taxon substitution
- Rewilding baselines: why the Pleistocene? Why not?
- De-extinction technologies (cloning, gene editing, back-breeding)
- Ecological, ethical, and practical objections
- The "Pleistocene Park" experiment (Sergey Zimov, Siberia)

## Unit 3.4: Passive Rewilding and Land Abandonment

### Readings

> Navarro, L.M., & Pereira, H.M. (2012). Rewilding abandoned landscapes in Europe. *Ecosystems*, 15(6), 900–912. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10021-012-9558-7
> - Key paper framing land abandonment as a rewilding opportunity

> Ceaușu, S., Hofmann, M., Navarro, L.M., Carver, S., Verburg, P.H., & Pereira, H.M. (2015). Mapping opportunities and challenges for rewilding in Europe. *Conservation Biology*, 29(4), 1017–1027.

> Perino, A., Pereira, H.M., Navarro, L.M., Fernández, N., Bullock, J.M., Ceaușu, S., ... & Wheeler, H.C. (2019). Rewilding complex ecosystems. *Science*, 364(6438), eaav5570. https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aav5570
> - **Essential**: Major synthesis arguing for rewilding as restoring ecological complexity

> Corlett, R.T. (2016). Restoration, reintroduction, and rewilding in a changing world. *Trends in Ecology & Evolution*, 31(6), 453–462. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tree.2016.02.017

### Key Concepts
- Passive vs. active rewilding
- Land abandonment trends (especially in Europe)
- Secondary succession and forest recovery
- Spontaneous rewilding
- Rewilding and ecosystem complexity (Perino et al., 2019)
- The trophic complexity framework: three dimensions of rewilding

### Exercise 3.3: Rewilding Case Study Analysis
Choose one active rewilding project worldwide. Conduct a comprehensive analysis:
1. **Ecological assessment**: What species/processes are being restored? What is the ecological rationale?
2. **Governance analysis**: Who is leading the project? What governance model is used? (Use your PSCI lens)
3. **Stakeholder mapping**: Identify key stakeholders and their positions
4. **Evidence review**: What monitoring data exists? What outcomes have been documented?
5. **Critical evaluation**: What are the strengths and weaknesses of this project?

## Unit 3.6: Measuring and Monitoring Rewilding

### Readings

> Pettorelli, N., Barlow, J., Stephens, P.A., Durant, S.M., Connor, B., Schulte to Bühne, H., ... & du Toit, J.T. (2018). Making rewilding fit for policy. *Journal of Applied Ecology*, 55(3), 1114–1125. https://doi.org/10.1111/1365-2664.13082

> Torres, A., Fernández, N., zu Ermgassen, S., Helmer, W., Revilla, E., Saavedra, D., ... & Pereira, H.M. (2018). Measuring rewilding progress. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*, 373(1761), 20170433. https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.2017.0433

> Carver, S., Convery, I., Hawkins, S., Beyers, R., Eagle, A., Kun, Z., ... & Fisher, M. (2021). Guiding principles for rewilding. *Conservation Biology*, 35(6), 1882–1893.

### Key Concepts
- Indicators and metrics for rewilding success
- Ecological integrity indices
- Wildness indices
- Wildlife population monitoring methods
- Remote sensing for rewilding monitoring
- Adaptive management frameworks
- The challenge of defining "success" in open-ended rewilding

### Exercise 3.4: Designing a Rewilding Monitoring Framework
Drawing on Torres et al. (2018) and Pettorelli et al. (2018):
1. Select a hypothetical or real rewilding site
2. Define measurable ecological objectives (e.g., trophic complexity, connectivity, species presence)
3. Design a monitoring protocol: what data would you collect, how often, with what methods?
4. How would you use your data science skills? (Consider: remote sensing, camera traps + ML, eDNA, acoustic monitoring)
5. Define criteria for "success" at 5, 10, and 25 years

**Deliverable**: A monitoring framework document (2,000 words) with data collection plan.

- Is rewilding "giving up" on degraded ecosystems?

- Rewilding and environmental justice
- Can rewilding work in the Global South, or is it a Northern concept?
