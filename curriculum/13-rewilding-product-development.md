# Module 13: Personal Rewilding Product — Concept to MVP

**Type**: Tier 4 applied capstone  
**Estimated time**: 120–200 hours  
**Prerequisites**: Modules 1–10 complete, Module 12 complete  
**Assessment**: Product specification + working prototype + user testing report + launch plan  
**Benchmark mapping**: Beyond standard ecology master's; applied product development

---

## Purpose

This module scaffolds the creation of a personal rewilding product that combines your ecological training with practical application. The product should:

- Be genuinely useful to people trying to support biodiversity in their own spaces
- Scale gracefully from apartment balconies to multi-acre properties
- Combine botany, ecology, and practical gardening in a way that existing products don't
- Use data and technology to deliver personalized, ecologically grounded guidance
- Generate modest passive income while doing real ecological good

**You do not need to have the product figured out before starting.** This module is designed to help you discover and validate the right product through structured exploration. Your ecological training is the differentiator—lean into it.

---

## Design Principles

These principles should guide every decision throughout the module. Return to them often.

### 1. Ecological Rigor Meets Accessibility
The product must be grounded in real ecology (not gardening folklore), but communicated in language anyone can act on. A beginner should feel empowered, not overwhelmed. An expert should feel respected, not patronized.

### 2. Scale-Adaptive by Design
A container garden on a balcony and a 50-acre rural property are fundamentally different ecological contexts. Your product should recognize this and adapt its recommendations accordingly:

| Scale | Ecological Character | Product Implications |
|---|---|---|
| **Container / balcony** (0–50 sq ft) | Microhabitat; pollinator support focus; high management intensity | Curated plant palettes, simple tracking, high guidance |
| **Small urban yard** (50–2,000 sq ft) | Patch ecology; habitat islands; edge effects dominate | Native garden design, soil health, neighbor-facing aesthetics |
| **Suburban yard** (2,000 sq ft – 1 acre) | Meaningful habitat connectivity possible; lawn conversion | Phased rewilding plans, meadow establishment, water features |
| **Large lot / smallholding** (1–10 acres) | Landscape-scale processes begin; succession management | Habitat mosaic design, less micromanagement, monitoring frameworks |
| **Rural property** (10+ acres) | Ecosystem management; conservation easement potential | Restoration ecology principles, minimal intervention, wildlife corridors |

### 3. Data-Informed, Not Data-Demanding
Track meaningful ecological outcomes without requiring users to become field ecologists. Automate where possible, simplify where automation isn't feasible.

### 4. Public Good as Core Value
The product should generate value for biodiversity, not just for the user's yard. Aggregated data, community science contributions, and shared knowledge are features, not afterthoughts.

## Phase 1: Problem Discovery (Weeks 1–4)

### 1.1 Synthesize Your Curriculum Knowledge

Before looking outward, look inward. Review your notes, deliverables, and reflections from the entire curriculum. Answer these questions in writing (2–3 pages):

- What ecological principles are most underserved in consumer products? (Draw on Modules 1–3)
- What data and modeling tools could be adapted for non-specialist use? (Draw on Modules 4–6)
- What aspects of climate adaptation are most actionable at the individual level? (Draw on Module 7)
- What governance or collective action insights apply to community-level ecological products? (Draw on Module 8)
- What computational tools from your Tier 2 work could power a consumer product? (Draw on tracks)
- What did your Module 12 market landscape reveal as the biggest unmet needs?

### 1.2 User Research

Talk to real people. You need at minimum **15 conversations** across these segments:

| Segment | Target Count | Key Questions |
|---|---|---|
| Enthusiastic beginners (want to help nature, don't know how) | 4–5 | What stops you? What have you tried? What would make it easy? |
| Experienced native plant gardeners | 3–4 | What tools do you wish existed? What data do you track? What frustrates you? |
| Professional landscapers / restoration practitioners | 2–3 | What would make native/ecological work easier to sell to clients? |
| Smart home / IoT enthusiasts | 2–3 | What ecological data would interest you? What integrations matter? |
| Landowners with acreage | 2–3 | What's your relationship with your land ecology? What would help? |

**Interview protocol**:
1. Open-ended questions first (don't lead)
2. Show them existing products — what do they like/dislike?
3. Describe 2–3 rough concepts and gauge reaction
4. Ask about willingness to pay and preferred format

### 1.3 Problem Statement

Synthesize your research into a crisp problem statement:

> **[Target user segment]** wants to **[ecological goal]** but currently can't because **[specific barriers]**. Existing solutions fail because **[gap]**. A product that **[key differentiator]** would unlock **[specific outcome]**.

Write three versions for three different user segments. Select one as your primary focus (you can serve others later).

### Deliverables — Phase 1
- Curriculum synthesis document (2–3 pages)
- 15+ user interview summaries (1 paragraph each)
- Three problem statements with rationale for selection
- Updated "opportunity map" from Module 12

## Phase 2: Concept Definition (Weeks 5–8)

### 2.1 Product Concept Exploration

Based on your problem discovery, develop **three distinct product concepts** to at least sketch depth. Use the [Product Ideation Worksheets](../resources/product-ideation-worksheets.md) to structure each.

**Concept archetypes to consider** (these are starting points, not requirements — your research may reveal better directions):

#### Archetype A: Intelligent Native Plant Guide + Tracker
A mobile/web app that uses location, soil type, sun exposure, property size, and ecological goals to recommend native plant palettes — then tracks what you plant, monitors ecological outcomes over time, and adapts recommendations seasonally.

*Ecological basis*: Species distribution modeling (Module 4), plant-insect networks (Module 1), community assembly theory (Restoration Track)  
*Data sources*: USDA plant database, BONAP, ecoregion maps, local phenology data, iNaturalist observations  
*Differentiator*: Recommendations grounded in ecological niche theory, not just "what grows here"

#### Archetype B: Backyard Biodiversity Dashboard  
A monitoring system (optional hardware sensors + software) that helps users track ecological health of their space over time — species observed, pollinator visits, soil health indicators, vegetation cover — with benchmarking against what's ecologically possible for their location.

*Ecological basis*: Biodiversity monitoring (Module 5), landscape metrics (Module 4), functional ecology (Module 1)  
*Data sources*: User observations + optional iNaturalist integration, sensor data (soil moisture, temperature), eBird/Budburst  
*Differentiator*: Outcome tracking, not just activity tracking. "Your yard supports X% of potential native pollinator species."

#### Archetype C: Scale-Adaptive Rewilding Planner  
A planning tool that generates phased rewilding/restoration plans based on property characteristics, ecological region, user commitment level, and budget. Small spaces get container and border recommendations; large properties get habitat mosaic designs with succession timelines.

*Ecological basis*: Restoration ecology (Restoration Track), succession theory (Module 1), landscape connectivity (Module 4), adaptive management (Module 3)  
*Data sources*: Soils data, climate data, land cover data, native plant databases, user property info  
*Differentiator*: Ecologically-principled plans that adapt to scale, not generic "plant these 10 natives" lists

#### Archetype D: Ecological Smart Garden System  
Hardware + software combining soil/moisture/light sensors with ecological intelligence. Unlike existing smart garden products that optimize for crop yield, this system optimizes for biodiversity outcomes — recommending native plant placements, watering schedules that mimic natural hydrology, and management actions based on ecological phenology.

*Ecological basis*: Ecophysiology, phenology (Module 7), soil ecology (Restoration Track), microhabitat management  
*Data sources*: Real-time sensor feeds + weather APIs + phenology databases + native plant requirements  
*Differentiator*: Ecological intelligence in hardware; smart home integration with conservation goals

#### Archetype E: Community Rewilding Platform  
A neighborhood or regional platform where users contribute their property to a collective rewilding effort. The platform maps participating properties as habitat patches, identifies connectivity gaps, suggests corridor plantings, and tracks aggregate biodiversity data.

*Ecological basis*: Metapopulation theory (Module 1), landscape connectivity (Module 4), collective action (Module 8), conservation planning  
*Data sources*: User property data, land cover maps, species occurrence data, connectivity models  
*Differentiator*: Turns individual yard-scale actions into landscape-scale conservation strategy

#### Archetype F: Something You Discover  
Your user research and curriculum knowledge may reveal a product direction not listed here. That's ideal — it means you've found something the scaffolding author didn't anticipate.

### 2.2 Concept Scoring Matrix

Evaluate your three concepts against these criteria:

| Criterion | Weight | Concept 1 | Concept 2 | Concept 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ecological impact potential | 20% | | | |
| Addresses validated user pain point | 20% | | | |
| Feasible with your current skills | 15% | | | |
| Passive income potential | 10% | | | |
| Beginner accessibility | 10% | | | |
| Expert depth | 10% | | | |
| Scale adaptability | 10% | | | |
| Data / network effects | 5% | | | |

Score 1–5 for each. Weight and sum. But don't just pick the highest score — use it as input to your judgment, not a replacement for it.

### 2.3 Concept Selection and Refinement

Select one concept. Write a **Product Concept Document** (3–5 pages):

1. **Problem statement** (refined from Phase 1)
2. **Product vision** — What does the world look like if this succeeds?
3. **Core features** (MVP scope — ruthlessly minimal)
4. **User personas** (at least 2: one beginner, one experienced)
5. **Ecological differentiators** — What ecological knowledge makes this better than a non-ecologist could build?
6. **Scale adaptation plan** — How does the product work at each scale tier?
7. **Data strategy** — What data powers it, where does it come from, what does the user contribute?
8. **Revenue model** (from Module 12 framework)
9. **Key risks and unknowns**

### Deliverables — Phase 2
- Three concept sketches (1–2 pages each, using ideation worksheets)
- Scoring matrix with commentary
- Product Concept Document (3–5 pages)

## Phase 3: Ecological Architecture (Weeks 9–12)

This phase is where your curriculum investment pays off most directly. You're designing the ecological engine that powers the product.

### 3.1 Ecological Knowledge Model

Define the ecological knowledge your product needs to encode:

- **Species selection logic**: How do you match plants to conditions? What variables matter? (Draw on SDM methods, Module 4)
- **Ecological interaction data**: What plant-pollinator, plant-bird, or plant-soil relationships does the product need to know about? (Draw on community ecology, Module 1)
- **Succession and management rules**: What happens over time? When should users intervene vs. let things be? (Draw on restoration ecology, Module 3 and Restoration Track)
- **Regional calibration**: How do recommendations differ by ecoregion? (Draw on biogeography, Module 1; climate projections, Module 7)
- **Scale-dependent logic**: What ecological processes dominate at which scales? (Draw on landscape ecology, Module 4)

### 3.2 Data Pipeline Design

Map out the data sources and how they flow into product features:

```
[Data Sources]                    [Processing]                 [Product Features]
─────────────                    ────────────                 ──────────────────
USDA Plants DB         ──┐
BONAP range data       ──┼──→  Species filtering     ──→  Plant recommendations
Ecoregion maps         ──┘      by location + conditions

Climate normals        ──┐
PRISM / Daymet         ──┼──→  Climate matching      ──→  Hardiness + phenology
Climate projections    ──┘      and future-proofing        guidance

iNaturalist API        ──┐
eBird API              ──┼──→  Local biodiversity    ──→  Baseline assessment
GBIF                   ──┘      context                    + progress tracking

User property data     ──┐
Soil surveys (SSURGO)  ──┼──→  Site characterization ──→  Personalized plans
Sun/shade assessment   ──┘

User observations      ──┐
Sensor data (optional) ──┼──→  Outcome tracking      ──→  Biodiversity dashboard
Photo monitoring       ──┘                                 + community data
```

### 3.3 Algorithm Sketches

For each core product feature, write a pseudocode or flowchart description of the ecological logic. Examples:

**Native plant recommendation engine**:
```
INPUT: lat/lon, soil_type, sun_exposure, moisture, property_size, user_goals
1. Determine ecoregion and plant hardiness zone
2. Query native species list for ecoregion
3. Filter by site conditions (soil, sun, moisture tolerance ranges)
4. Score by ecological value:
   - Host plant relationships (Lepidoptera spp. supported)
   - Pollinator attraction (bloom period coverage)
   - Bird habitat value (fruit, nesting, cover)
   - Soil ecology contributions (N-fixation, mycorrhizal associations)
5. Apply scale-appropriate selection:
   - Small: prioritize high-value-per-area species
   - Medium: add structural diversity layers
   - Large: include matrix and succession species
6. Generate seasonal planting calendar
7. Provide companion planting clusters (ecological guilds)
OUTPUT: Ranked plant list with rationale, planting plan, care schedule
```

**Biodiversity progress tracker**:
```
INPUT: user_observations (species, date, count), property_baseline
1. Calculate observed species richness 
2. Compare to expected richness for ecoregion + habitat type
3. Track functional group coverage:
   - Native forbs, grasses, shrubs, trees represented?
   - Bloom period gaps in pollinator resources?
   - Structural habitat layers present?
4. Generate progress metrics:
   - % of target native species palette established
   - Pollinator season coverage score
   - Habitat complexity index
5. Identify gaps and recommend next additions
OUTPUT: Dashboard with progress scores, trend graphs, action items
```

### 3.4 Technical Stack Decision

Based on your computational skills (Python, R, ML, data science) and the product concept, decide on a technical architecture. Considerations:

| Component | Options to Evaluate |
|---|---|
| Backend | Python (FastAPI/Django), Node.js, serverless (AWS Lambda) |
| Database | PostgreSQL + PostGIS (spatial), SQLite (local-first), Firebase |
| Frontend | React/Next.js, Flutter (mobile), simple PWA |
| Data pipeline | Python scripts, Airflow, simple cron jobs |
| ML/modeling | scikit-learn, PyMC for Bayesian components, pre-computed lookup tables |
| Hosting | Vercel, Railway, AWS, self-hosted |
| Sensors (if applicable) | ESP32 + MQTT, off-the-shelf consumer sensors with API |

**Recommendation for MVP**: Start with the simplest architecture that validates your core ecological logic. A well-designed spreadsheet or static site that demonstrates the recommendation engine is better than a half-built app.

### Deliverables — Phase 3
- Ecological knowledge model document
- Data pipeline diagram
- Algorithm sketches for core features (pseudocode or flowchart)
- Technical architecture decision record

## Phase 4: Prototype and Test (Weeks 13–18)

### 4.1 MVP Definition

Define the **Minimum Viable Product** — the smallest thing you can build that tests whether your core ecological value proposition works for real users.

**MVP is NOT**:
- A polished app
- A complete feature set
- Hardware (unless hardware IS the product)
- Something that requires a large user base to function

**MVP IS**:
- A working version of ONE core feature
- Enough to put in front of 5–10 users
- Something that demonstrates ecological intelligence
- Something you can build in 4–6 weeks

**MVP examples by archetype**:
- **Plant guide**: A web tool where users enter their zip code and conditions, and get a ranked native plant list with ecological rationale
- **Biodiversity dashboard**: A spreadsheet template + simple visualization that tracks observations against a regional baseline
- **Rewilding planner**: A guided questionnaire that produces a phased PDF plan for the user's specific property
- **Smart garden**: Software prototype using simulated sensor data, with hardware deferred
- **Community platform**: A neighborhood pilot with a shared map and data collection form

### 4.2 Build Sprint

Allocate 4–6 weeks of focused building time. Structure it:

**Week 1**: Core data integration
- Get your primary data sources loaded and queryable
- Build the basic location → ecoregion → species pipeline

**Weeks 2–3**: Core ecological logic
- Implement your recommendation/scoring/tracking algorithm
- Validate against known ecological ground truth (does it recommend sensible plants for your own location?)

**Week 4**: User interface (minimal)
- Build enough UI for someone else to use it without you standing over their shoulder
- Focus on clarity, not beauty

**Weeks 5–6**: Testing and iteration
- Put it in front of users (see 4.3)
- Fix the worst problems
- Document what works and what doesn't

### 4.3 User Testing Protocol

Recruit 8–12 testers from your Phase 1 interview pool (or new participants). Structure testing:

1. **Task-based testing** (30 min): Give users specific tasks ("Find native plants for your front yard") and observe
2. **Think-aloud protocol**: Ask users to verbalize their thoughts as they use the product
3. **Ecological accuracy check**: Have 2–3 ecologically knowledgeable testers evaluate whether recommendations are sound
4. **Post-test interview** (15 min):
   - What was useful?
   - What was confusing?
   - Would you use this again? Would you pay for it?
   - What's missing?

### 4.4 Iteration Log

Keep a structured log:

| Date | Feedback Source | Issue | Ecological Accuracy? | Action Taken |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | |

### Deliverables — Phase 4
- Working MVP
- User testing report (10–15 user sessions documented)
- Iteration log
- Revised product concept document (reflecting what you learned)

## Phase 5: Business Viability and Launch Planning (Weeks 19–24)

### 5.1 Revenue Validation

Based on user testing, refine your revenue model:

- **Who would pay?** (Which user segment showed strongest willingness?)
- **What would they pay for?** (Which features had the most pull?)
- **How much?** (Test specific price points in follow-up conversations)
- **What's the acquisition channel?** (How did testers find out about ecological products currently?)

### 5.2 Passive Income Architecture

Design for passive income from the start:

| Revenue Stream | Effort Model | Passive Potential |
|---|---|---|
| One-time app purchase | Build once, maintain periodically | High |
| Subscription (content/data updates) | Regular content creation needed | Medium |
| Affiliate links (nurseries, tools) | Setup + periodic review | High |
| Data licensing (aggregated, anonymized) | Automatic once pipeline exists | High |
| Premium features / pro tier | Build once, gate access | High |
| Physical product kits (seed mixes, sensor bundles) | Fulfillment needed or dropship | Medium |
| API access for other developers | Build once, document | High |
| Educational content (guides, courses) | Create once, sell indefinitely | High |

### 5.3 Launch Plan

Write a launch plan covering:

1. **Pre-launch** (4–8 weeks before)
   - Set up landing page
   - Build email list from testing participants and interested communities
   - Identify 3–5 communities where your target users gather (Reddit r/NativePlantGardening, r/Permaculture, Wild Ones chapters, native plant societies, etc.)
   - Prepare 2–3 pieces of content demonstrating ecological expertise

2. **Soft launch**
   - Release to email list and testing community
   - Monitor for critical bugs and ecological accuracy issues
   - Collect initial reviews and testimonials

3. **Public launch**
   - Post to relevant communities
   - Reach out to native plant / rewilding influencers and bloggers
   - Consider Product Hunt, Hacker News, or ecology forums

4. **Post-launch iteration**
   - Track usage metrics
   - Maintain feedback loop
   - Plan v1.1 based on real usage patterns

### 5.4 Sustainability and Mission Alignment

Write a 1-page "Mission Lock" document:

- What ecological outcomes does this product commit to supporting?
- What would you never compromise on for revenue?
- How will you measure ecological impact (not just business impact)?
- What's your plan if the product becomes more profitable but less ecologically useful?

### Deliverables — Phase 5
- Revenue validation summary
- Passive income architecture document
- Launch plan
- Mission Lock document

## Phase 6: Post-Launch Learning Loop (Ongoing)

### 6.1 Metrics to Track

**Business metrics**:
- Users (active, retained)
- Revenue (MRR if subscription, one-time if purchase)
- Acquisition source
- Feature usage

**Ecological impact metrics** (these matter more):
- Square footage / acreage under ecologically-informed management
- Native species planted (estimated from recommendations followed)
- User-reported wildlife observations
- Community science data contributed
- Regional biodiversity data coverage

### 6.2 Continuous Ecological Improvement

Your product's ecological intelligence should improve over time:

- **User outcome data** → Refine recommendation algorithms
- **Climate projections updates** → Adjust forward-looking guidance (Module 7)
- **New research** → Update plant-pollinator interaction data, succession models
- **Community contributions** → Crowdsourced local ecological knowledge

### 6.3 Growth Pathways

Once v1.0 is stable, consider:

- **Geographic expansion** (new ecoregions, international)
- **Depth expansion** (more sophisticated monitoring, advanced user tools)
- **Partnership opportunities** (nurseries, conservation organizations, municipalities)
- **B2B pivot** (landscape architects, HOAs, land trusts, municipal parks departments)
- **Hardware integration** (if software-first; or software layer for others' hardware)
- **Open data contribution** (publish anonymized aggregate data as citizen science)

## Product Development Journal

Throughout all phases, maintain a **product development journal** in your repository. This is different from your capstone research log — it's a record of your evolving thinking about the product.

Suggested format:
```markdown
## [Date] — [Phase] — [Topic]
**Question**: What I was trying to figure out
**What I did**: Research, prototype, conversation, etc.
**What I learned**: Key insight
**Decision**: What I'm going to do about it
**Curriculum connection**: Which module/concept informed this
```

This journal serves three purposes:
1. Tracks your learning about product development
2. Creates content you can repurpose (blog posts, documentation)
3. Connects your ecological training to practical application
