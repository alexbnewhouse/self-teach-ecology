# Career Transition Guide: From PSCI to Ecological Data Science

**Type**: Resource document  
**When to use**: Revisit throughout the curriculum, especially during Tier 2 and before/during the capstone  
**Purpose**: Practical guidance on positioning a Political Science PhD with data science skills for research and labor in restoration ecology, rewilding, and conservation data science

---

## The Honest Assessment

You are making a nonstandard career move. That's an advantage, not a liability — but only if you can articulate why. Here's the reality:

**What works for you**:
- The conservation field is desperate for quantitative people who can also think about governance, institutions, and human behavior
- Your complex systems background maps directly onto ecological systems thinking (regime shifts, cascading failures, network dynamics, nonlinear feedbacks)
- Your ML/DS skills are scarce in ecology — most ecologists learn statistics reluctantly; few can build production ML pipelines
- Political ecology is increasingly relevant as conservation confronts justice, legitimacy, and governance challenges
- You can bridge the "social" and "ecological" in social-ecological systems — most researchers can only do one side

**What you'll need to overcome**:
- Ecology is a field with strong gatekeeping around field experience and organismmal knowledge (plant ID, bird ID, etc.)
- Some hiring managers will undervalue a PSCI PhD for ecological roles; you'll need a portfolio that proves ecological competence
- You'll need to learn the language and culture of ecology — how ecologists think, argue, and review each other's work
- Conservation organizations move slowly and pay poorly relative to tech; set expectations
- "Political ecologist who does data science" is not a standard job title — you may need to create your niche

## Three Career Trajectories

### Trajectory A: Conservation Data Scientist / ML Engineer
**Organizations**: Wildlife Conservation Society (WCS), The Nature Conservancy (TNC), WWF, Rainforest Alliance, Conservation International, World Resources Institute, Wildlife Insights, Allen Institute for AI (AI2)  
**Tech**: Microsoft AI for Good, Google DeepMind Environmental, Planetary Computer team, Earthrise Media  
**Government**: USGS, NOAA, EPA data teams, NASA DEVELOP  
**Academic adjacents**: Map of Life (Yale/Jetz Lab), Half-Earth Project, RESOLVE

**The role**: Build and maintain ML pipelines for conservation — camera trap analysis, satellite change detection, species distribution models, conservation planning optimization, biodiversity monitoring dashboards.

**What they look for**:
- Strong ML/DL portfolio (Python, PyTorch/TF)
- Experience with geospatial data at scale
- At least basic ecological literacy (species groups, conservation concepts, protected areas)
- Ability to communicate with ecologists and policy people
- Published or open-source ecological data science work

**Your edge**: You can do the ML *and* understand the governance/policy context that shapes what conservation decisions the ML is informing. Most conservation data scientists can't do the policy side.

**How to get there**:
1. Build 2–3 strong portfolio projects (Computational Ecology Pathway F exercises are designed for this)
2. Contribute to open-source conservation tech (MegaDetector, Wildlife Insights, eBird tools)
3. Present at ESA (Ecological Society of America) in the data science / conservation technology sessions
4. Apply for Microsoft AI for Good grants, Google.org fellowships, or Schmidt Futures positions

### Trajectory B: Social-Ecological Systems Researcher
**Organizations**: Stockholm Resilience Centre, SESYNC (Socio-Environmental Synthesis Center), Santa Fe Institute, Beijer Institute, Ostrom Workshop (Indiana)  
**Academic departments**: Environmental social science, sustainability science, coupled human-natural systems  
**Think tanks**: Resources for the Future, International Institute for Sustainable Development, IUCN governance teams

**The role**: Research the coupled dynamics of human institutions and ecological systems. Model how governance structures affect ecological outcomes and how ecological change drives institutional adaptation.

**What they look for**:
- Strong theoretical grounding in both social science and ecology
- Network analysis, ABM, or other complexity methods
- Publication record (working toward it in your case)
- Ability to work in interdisciplinary teams
- Genuine fluency in both ecological and social science literatures

**Your edge**: Your political violence work through a complex systems lens is *directly* transferable to social-ecological cascading dynamics. You understand nonlinear systems, threshold effects, and institutional collapse — these are exactly the same conceptual challenges in SES research.

**How to get there**:
1. Complete the Political Ecology track and Systems Ecology track
2. Do a capstone that explicitly bridges PSCI and ecology (social-ecological network analysis of a conservation/restoration governance case)
3. Target SESYNC working groups or Stockholm Resilience Centre visiting researcher positions
4. Publish at least one paper in an SES-relevant journal (Ecology and Society, Global Environmental Change, Sustainability Science)

### Trajectory C: Political Ecologist with Quantitative Methods
**Organizations**: Academic positions in geography, environmental studies, or sustainability science departments  
**Research institutes**: STEPS Centre (Sussex), Institute for Development Studies, CIFOR-ICRAF  
**NGO research**: IUCN CEESP (Commission on Environmental, Economic and Social Policy), Rights and Resources Initiative

**The role**: Do political ecology research — analyze the power dynamics, justice implications, and governance challenges of conservation and restoration — but bring quantitative and computational methods that most political ecologists lack.

**What they look for**:
- Deep knowledge of political ecology literature and theory
- Fieldwork experience (even limited — but some)
- Ability to publish in political ecology journals (Geoforum, Political Geography, Journal of Peasant Studies, Annals of the AAG)
- Mixed methods capability — quant + qual
- Engagement with justice and equity dimensions of environmental work

**Your edge**: Most political ecologists are qualitative researchers. A political ecologist who can run network analysis, NLP, spatial analysis, and ML is extremely rare and valuable. Your political violence background adds another layer — conservation conflict, green militarism, and securitization of nature are active research areas.

**How to get there**:
1. Complete the Political Ecology track — you need to know the canon
2. Do at least one qualitative exercise (interview-based, archival, or ethnographic — even a small one)
3. Do an NLP or network analysis project on conservation governance data (Exercise D.1 or D.2 from the Political Ecology track)
4. Submit to political ecology journals and conferences (AAG, PEWS, Dimensions of Political Ecology)
5. Position your PSCI PhD + ecology curriculum as interdisciplinary strength, not career switching

## Building Ecological Credibility

### The Portfolio Problem
Ecology, more than most fields, values demonstrated experience with real ecological systems. A portfolio of purely synthetic or textbook-data analyses will be viewed skeptically. Here's how to build credibility:

1. **Contribute to citizen science — seriously**
   - Become a skilled iNaturalist observer. Upload regularly. Get identifications confirmed.
   - Contribute to eBird (if interested in birds). The data quality requirements will teach you about field survey methodology.
   - This is not just resume padding — it builds organismal knowledge that ecologists consider foundational.

2. **Volunteer for restoration work**
   - Join a local native plant society or watershed group
   - Volunteer with a restoration organization doing planting, invasive removal, or monitoring
   - Document your experience and connect it to your curriculum learning
   - This gets you field hours and contacts

3. **Use real data in your projects**
   - Every exercise in this curriculum is designed around real data (NEON, GBIF, eBird, GEE). Do not substitute simulated data.
   - Publish your analyses on GitHub. Make them findable.

4. **Get at least one ecological publication**
   - Co-author with an ecologist if possible
   - Your capstone should target a real journal
   - Consider computational ecology or conservation technology journals where your methods skills are the main contribution

5. **Learn to talk about organisms**
   - You don't need to be a taxonomist, but you should be able to walk outside and identify 20+ common plant species in your region, know the common birds, and talk about local ecological processes
   - This is cultural capital in ecology — it signals you take the natural world seriously

## Positioning Your PSCI PhD

When you apply for ecological roles, your PSCI PhD will raise a question: "Why is this person here?" Have a clear, concise answer.

### The Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)
> "I study complex systems — I did my PhD on political violence dynamics using network analysis and computational modeling. I realized the same methods and frameworks apply to ecological systems: cascading failures, institutional governance, nonlinear dynamics. I spent [X months/years] building formal ecological expertise through a structured curriculum benchmarked against leading ecology master's programs, with a focus on conservation data science and political ecology. I bring quantitative methods that most ecologists need but few have, and governance analysis that most data scientists can't do."

### The Cover Letter Framework
1. Open with your specific interest in *their* work (not generic)
2. Explain the PSCI → ecology bridge in 2–3 sentences (complex systems transferability)
3. Point to your ecological portfolio (capstone, publications, GitHub repos)
4. Highlight your unique combination: ML + policy + ecology
5. Close with what you can offer that a standard ecology graduate can't

### What NOT to Say
- "I'm transitioning out of political science" (sounds like you're fleeing)
- "I taught myself ecology" (sounds amateur)
- Instead: "I built formal ecological expertise through a structured curriculum" or "I completed graduate-equivalent training in conservation ecology"

## Year-by-Year Roadmap (During Curriculum)

### Year 1: Foundation + Credibility Building
- Complete Tiers 0–1 and begin Tier 2
- Join ESA and one other society
- Start regular iNaturalist/eBird contributions
- Volunteer with a local restoration or native plant organization
- Attend ESA Annual Meeting (poster or observer)

### Year 2: Specialization + Output
- Complete Tier 2 (including Political Ecology track and Computational Ecology with Pathway F)
- Complete capstone targeting publication
- Submit first ecological publication
- Present at ESA or SER
- Apply for relevant grants or fellowships
- Build 3+ strong GitHub portfolio projects

### Year 3+: Professional Entry
- Target positions from trajectory A, B, or C
- Network at conferences — have specific conversations with specific people
- Apply for postdoctoral fellowships if research-track
- Or apply for data scientist / research analyst positions at conservation organizations
- Continue publishing to build ecological publication record
