# From Complex Systems to Ecological Systems: A Concept Bridge

**Purpose**: Map your existing PSCI complex-systems knowledge onto ecological equivalents. This document helps you see where your training already applies and where ecological concepts extend beyond your current framework.

---

## How to Use This Document

Each section below takes a concept you already understand from political science and complex systems and maps it to the ecological version. The "Translation Notes" explain where the mapping is tight and where it breaks down — the breakdowns are where the real learning happens.

---

## 1. Network Dynamics

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Social network analysis of political actors (alliance formation, brokerage, centrality as power)
- Armed group networks and fragmentation (Bakke, Cunningham & Loyle)
- Institutional linkages and policy diffusion
- Conflict contagion and diffusion across networks

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Food webs**: Directed networks where edges are energy/nutrient flows between trophic levels. Centrality ≈ keystone species importance. Node removal ≈ species extinction cascading through the web.
- **Mutualistic networks**: Plant-pollinator or seed-dispersal networks. Nestedness ≈ a structural property (unlike most social networks). "Rewiring" after species loss ≈ adaptation after actor elimination.
- **Social-ecological networks (SEN)**: Multi-layer networks with social nodes (actors, institutions) and ecological nodes (habitat patches, species). This is where your PSCI and ecology skills converge directly.
- **Landscape connectivity**: Patches as nodes, corridors/dispersal routes as edges. Graph-theoretic metrics (e.g., Probability of Connectivity) borrowed directly from network science.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: Centrality → keystone importance; cascade dynamics; fragmentation effects. Your intuition about network robustness translates almost 1:1.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecological networks have much stronger physical constraints (organisms can't "choose" partners like political actors). Evolution shapes network structure over millennia, not just strategic choice. Ecological networks have strict energetic accounting (thermodynamics) with no social equivalent.
- **Key reading**: Dunne, J.A. et al. (2002) "Food-web structure and network theory"; Sayles, J.S. et al. (2019) "Social-ecological network analysis of scale mismatches in estuary watershed restoration"

## 2. Regime Shifts and Tipping Points

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Political regime transitions (authoritarian → democratic, stable → conflict)
- Critical junctures and path dependence (Mahoney, Pierson)
- Threshold models of collective action (Granovetter)
- Civil war onset as phase transition
- Nonlinear dynamics of escalation and de-escalation

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Alternative stable states**: Ecosystems can exist in multiple equilibrium states (clear lake / turbid lake; forest / grassland). Transitions are abrupt, nonlinear, and often irreversible — just like political regime shifts.
- **Regime shifts**: Lake eutrophication, coral reef collapse, savanna-forest transitions, desertification. Formally the same concept: the system crosses a threshold and reorganizes around a new attractor.
- **Hysteresis**: Once a system shifts, it doesn't shift back at the same parameter value. You need *more* restoration effort than the original disturbance to return to the original state. This is why rewilding is hard and expensive.
- **Early warning signals**: Rising variance, critical slowing down, flickering. These are being used to detect impending ecological collapse — they're also being explored for predicting political instability (same math).
- **Resilience**: The capacity to absorb disturbance without shifting to an alternative state. In PSCI, think institutional resilience. In ecology, think ecosystem resilience. Same systems-theoretic concept.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: This is perhaps the strongest conceptual bridge. Scheffer's "Critical Transitions" (2009) draws explicitly on the same dynamical systems theory used in conflict studies. If you understand bifurcation diagrams, you already understand regime shifts.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecological regime shifts involve physical/chemical thresholds (phosphorus loading, temperature) that are more precisely measurable than most political thresholds. However, the uncertainty and irreversibility messages are identical.
- **Key reading**: Scheffer, M. (2009) *Critical Transitions in Nature and Society*; Scheffer et al. (2001) "Catastrophic shifts in ecosystems"; Biggs et al. (2018) "Regime Shifts" chapter in *The Oxford Handbook of Social-Ecological Resilience*

## 3. Institutional Analysis and Governance

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Institutional design and collective action problems
- Principal-agent problems, regulatory capture
- Common-pool resource theory (you may know Ostrom already)
- International regimes and treaty compliance
- State capacity and enforcement

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Commons governance**: Ostrom's work on common-pool resources IS the ecology-governance bridge. Her SES (Social-Ecological Systems) framework is the standard interdisciplinary model.
- **Protected area governance**: The politics of who decides what gets protected, how enforcement works, and who bears the costs. Think of national parks as contested governance institutions.
- **CBNRM (Community-Based Natural Resource Management)**: Devolution of conservation authority to local communities. Successes and failures map directly onto PSCI theories of local governance.
- **Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES)**: Market-based instruments for conservation. Governance challenges include monitoring, additionality, leakage — classic institutional design problems.
- **Polycentric governance**: Ostrom's term, used in both PSCI and conservation. Multiple overlapping authorities managing resources at different scales.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: Almost 1:1. Ostrom is cited in both disciplines. If you've read *Governing the Commons*, you already have the bridge.
- **Where it diverges**: Conservation governance adds the "nature" dimension — the resource being governed has its own dynamics, can move (animals cross borders), can collapse nonlinearly, and doesn't negotiate. This makes conservation governance harder than many purely social governance problems.
- **Key reading**: Ostrom, E. (2009) "A general framework for analyzing sustainability of social-ecological systems"; Cox, M. et al. (2010) "A review of design principles for community-based natural resource management"

## 4. Conflict and Violence

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Civil war dynamics, rebel governance, political violence
- Securitization theory
- Resource curse and conflict resources
- Civilians in conflict
- State monopoly on violence and its failures

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Human-wildlife conflict**: When wildlife and human livelihoods collide — crop raiding by elephants, livestock predation by wolves. Governance solutions that look remarkably like peacebuilding: stakeholder engagement, compensation schemes, shared planning.
- **Conservation conflict**: Conflicts between conservation objectives and local livelihoods, Indigenous rights, or development goals. Fortress conservation vs. community-based approaches is a deeply political debate.
- **Green militarism / securitization of nature**: Military and paramilitary enforcement of conservation. Shoot-on-sight policies for poachers. The "war for biodiversity" — this directly connects to your securitization and political violence expertise.
- **Environmental peacebuilding**: Using environmental cooperation to build peace (e.g., transboundary conservation areas as peace parks). Your conflict background is directly applicable.
- **Environmental defenders**: Violence against people who resist resource extraction or conservation displacement. >200 killed per year. Power dynamics you understand from conflict studies.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: This is your most unique contribution. Very few people in ecology understand political violence theory. Green militarism, conservation conflict, and environmental peacebuilding are areas where your PSCI PhD gives you genuine expertise that ecologists lack.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecologists tend to view conflict as a management problem to be solved, not as a systemic feature of power relations. Your PSCI lens will help you see structural violence and power dynamics that ecologists often miss.
- **Key reading**: Duffy, R. (2014) "Waging a war to save biodiversity: the rise of militarised conservation"; Büscher & Ramutsindela (2016) "Green violence"; Lunstrum, E. (2014) "Green Militarization"

## 5. Causal Inference from Observational Data

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Natural experiments, instrumental variables, diff-in-diff
- Regression discontinuity
- Matching methods (propensity score, CEM)
- Process tracing and within-case analysis
- Selection bias, endogeneity, omitted variable bias

### Ecological Equivalent
- **BACI (Before-After-Control-Impact)**: The ecological equivalent of diff-in-diff. Used to assess impacts of interventions (dam construction, restoration, protected areas).
- **Quasi-experimental conservation evaluation**: Exactly your methods. Did this protected area actually reduce deforestation? Matching + diff-in-diff on spatial data. Andam et al. (2008) showed that naive estimates of park effectiveness were biased by 2x.
- **Counterfactual evaluation of restoration**: What would have happened without the restoration intervention? Same selection bias problems you know from PSCI — sites chosen for restoration are not random.
- **Spatial regression discontinuity**: Used at park boundaries to estimate conservation effects. Virtually identical methods to PSCI applications.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: The methods are identical. The econometric revolution hit ecology about a decade after PSCI. Your causal inference training is directly transferable and highly valued in ecology.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecological data has extreme spatial and temporal autocorrelation that makes standard errors unreliable. You'll need to learn spatial statistics and temporal autocorrelation handling. Also, ecological "treatment" effects often take decades to manifest.
- **Key reading**: Ferraro, P. & Hanauer, M. (2014) "Advances in measuring the environmental and social impacts of environmental programs"; Baylis et al. (2016) "Mainstreaming impact evaluation in nature conservation"

## 6. Agent-Based Modeling and Simulation

### What You Know (PSCI)
- ABMs of conflict dynamics, ethnic violence, or political polarization (Epstein, Cederman, Bruch & Atwell)
- Schelling-type segregation models
- Simulation as theory-building
- NetLogo / Mesa / custom implementations

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Individual-Based Models (IBMs)**: Same concept, ecology terminology. Organisms as agents with movement rules, resource acquisition, reproduction, mortality. Used for population viability analysis, disease spread, habitat use.
- **Social-ecological ABMs**: Agents include both human actors (farmers, fishers, managers) and ecological entities (animals, vegetation patches). This is THE integration point for PSCI and ecology.
- **Landscape simulation models**: Fire spread, vegetation succession, species colonization. Often spatially explicit on raster grids — you may know this from conflict event geography.
- **Adaptive management simulation**: Using ABMs to test management strategies before implementing them. Parallel to policy simulation in PSCI.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: If you can build a NetLogo or Mesa ABM of conflict dynamics, you can build one of predator-prey dynamics or land-use change with minimal retraining. The calibration and validation challenges are also the same.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecological IBMs often need much more realistic biophysics (metabolic rates, dispersal kernels, allometric scaling). The agents have less behavioral complexity but more physiological realism. Also, ecological IBMs are much more commonly used for management decisions — the stakes of "what does the model recommend" are higher.
- **Key reading**: Railsback & Grimm (2019) *Agent-Based and Individual-Based Modeling*; Schlüter et al. (2012) "New horizons for managing the environment: A review of coupled social-ecological systems modeling"

## 7. Spatial Analysis and GIS

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Georeferenced conflict event data (ACLED, UCDP-GED)
- Spatial statistics (spatial lag, spatial error models)
- Kernel density estimation for event data
- Possibly some remote sensing for damage assessment

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Species distribution modeling (SDM)**: Predicting where species occur based on environmental variables + occurrence records. MaxEnt, random forests, boosted regression trees on georeferenced data.
- **Landscape metrics**: Quantifying habitat fragmentation, connectivity, patch area. FRAGSTATS, graph-theoretic metrics.
- **Conservation planning**: Marxan, Zonation — spatial optimization to select reserve networks. Same algorithmic thinking as facility location problems.
- **Remote sensing for land cover**: Satellite classification of vegetation, land use change, deforestation detection. Your remote sensing exposure applies directly.
- **Movement ecology**: Tracking data (GPS collars) analyzed as spatial trajectories. Home range estimation, step-selection functions.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: Your spatial statistics background transfers well. The analytical frameworks are similar. CRS, projections, spatial joins — all the same.
- **Where it diverges**: Ecological spatial analysis is much more dependent on remote sensing and raster data than conflict event data. You'll need to build comfort with multispectral imagery, NDVI, GEE. Also, scale dependency is more extreme — ecological patterns change dramatically from 1m to 1km resolution.
- **Key reading**: Fletcher & Fortin (2018) *Spatial Ecology and Conservation Modeling*; Module 4 of this curriculum covers this thoroughly.

## 8. NLP and Text Analysis

### What You Know (PSCI)
- Sentiment analysis of political texts
- Topic modeling (LDA, structural topic models)
- Possibly transformer-based classification or NER
- Political speech or media analysis

### Ecological Equivalent
- **Conservation policy text analysis**: Mining CBD national reports, NDCs (Nationally Determined Contributions), IUCN Red List narratives, EIA documents for themes, commitments, and compliance.
- **Environmental discourse analysis**: How environmental issues are framed. "Fortress conservation" vs. "community-based conservation" in policy documents — you can trace this computationally.
- **Scientific literature mining**: NLP on ecological papers to identify research gaps, track paradigm shifts, or extract species-trait data from narrative descriptions.
- **Social media for conservation**: Analyzing iNaturalist comments, conservation NGO communications, or green conflict discourse on Twitter/X.

### Translation Notes
- **Tight mapping**: Method is identical. The corpus changes but the tools don't. This is an easy skill transfer.
- **Where it diverges**: The corpora are smaller and more specialized. Environmental policy language has its own jargon that requires domain-specific fine-tuning or dictionary creation. Also, multilingual analysis matters a lot — conservation is global.
- **Key reading**: Chowdhury et al. (2023) "A review of machine learning applications for the conservation of biodiversity"; Exercise D.2 in the Political Ecology track covers this

## Where Your PSCI Background Is Genuinely Novel

These are areas where your combination of expertise creates contributions that neither a pure ecologist nor a pure data scientist can make:

1. **Conservation conflict analysis using political violence methods**: Apply your conflict analysis toolkit (event coding, escalation dynamics, actor mapping) to conservation conflicts. Nobody else is doing this computationally.

2. **Governance network analysis of restoration programs**: Who has power in a restoration project? How do networks of authority shape ecological outcomes? Apply your PSCI network analysis to real restoration governance.

3. **Securitization and green militarization metrics**: Build quantitative measures of conservation militarization from policy documents, budgets, and enforcement data. Your political violence lens sees things ecologists miss.

4. **Causal inference for conservation policy evaluation**: Apply the rigorous causal identification strategies from PSCI econometrics to conservation interventions. The ecology world is still catching up on these methods.

5. **Modeling coupled political-ecological dynamics**: Build ABMs or network models where political actors and ecological processes interact — conflict-driven land use change affecting biodiversity, or ecological degradation triggering governance failure. This is frontier work.
